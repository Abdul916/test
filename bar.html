1. Introduction

The Sweet v. Cardona case represents a major legal victory for student borrowers in the U.S., particularly those who were defrauded by their educational institutions and left burdened with student debt. The case brought to light the challenges students faced when seeking debt relief through the Borrower Defense to Repayment (BDR) program, exposing systemic delays and inefficiencies within the Department of Education. This landmark lawsuit has reshaped how borrower defense claims are processed and has paved the way for thousands of students to have their loans forgiven, offering a crucial lifeline to those misled by predatory schools.

In this detailed exploration of Sweet v. Cardona, we will examine the background, key outcomes, and the broader implications for the borrower defense program and student loan policy.

2. The Background of Borrower Defense to Repayment

The Borrower Defense to Repayment (BDR) program was introduced by the U.S. Department of Education as a way for students to seek forgiveness of federal student loans if they could prove that their school had misled them or engaged in fraudulent practices. The program was established in 1994, but it gained prominence in the mid-2010s when several for-profit colleges, including Corinthian Colleges and ITT Technical Institute, were found to have misled students about job placement rates, accreditation, and the value of their degrees.

Under the BDR program, a student could apply for loan forgiveness by demonstrating that the school had engaged in misconduct that directly harmed them. While the program was a lifeline for students who had been defrauded, the processing of claims became a significant issue. Many students waited for years without receiving a decision on their claims, and others had their applications wrongfully denied with little to no explanation. These delays and inefficiencies formed the foundation of the Sweet v. Cardona lawsuit.

3. The Genesis of Sweet v. Cardona

Sweet v. Cardona began in 2019, originally filed as Sweet v. DeVos, named after then-U.S. Secretary of Education, Betsy DeVos. The lawsuit was brought by a group of borrowers who alleged that the Department of Education had failed to process their borrower defense claims in a timely and transparent manner. At the heart of the case were thousands of students who had filed BDR claims and had been left in financial limbo for years, without any decision or explanation from the Department of Education.

The lead plaintiffs, supported by the Project on Predatory Student Lending, sought legal recourse to force the Department to process their claims. The case grew in significance as it represented not only the interests of the plaintiffs but also those of over 200,000 other borrowers whose claims were similarly delayed.

The lawsuit contended that the Department of Education had violated the Administrative Procedure Act (APA) by failing to provide timely decisions on borrower defense claims. The APA mandates that federal agencies must provide decisions in a reasonable time frame and must explain the basis for those decisions, particularly when denying claims. The plaintiffs argued that the Department had failed on both counts, and the lawsuit aimed to hold the Department accountable for its mismanagement of the program.

4. The Shift from Sweet v. DeVos to Sweet v. Cardona

In 2021, as the Biden administration took office, Miguel Cardona replaced Betsy DeVos as Secretary of Education, leading to the renaming of the case to Sweet v. Cardona. This change in leadership at the Department of Education signaled a shift in policy toward borrower defense and student loan forgiveness.

While the lawsuit had originally been filed under the Trump administration, which had been accused of slowing down borrower defense claims, the new administration indicated a willingness to resolve the backlog and streamline the BDR process. This change in tone was welcomed by many student borrowers, who had long felt abandoned by the Department. However, despite the change in leadership, the plaintiffs continued to press for concrete action to ensure that their claims would be resolved.

5. Key Outcomes of the Sweet v. Cardona Settlement

In June 2022, the Sweet v. Cardona lawsuit reached a settlement that provided long-awaited relief for thousands of borrowers. The settlement, approved by a federal judge, outlined several key provisions that would change how the Department of Education handled borrower defense claims.

  • Automatic Loan Forgiveness for Certain Borrowers: One of the most significant outcomes of the settlement was that certain borrowers would receive automatic loan forgiveness. These were borrowers who had attended schools that were identified as having engaged in widespread fraud or misconduct, including Corinthian Colleges and ITT Technical Institute. These institutions had been the subject of numerous lawsuits and investigations, and the Department of Education acknowledged that their misconduct warranted automatic loan discharges for affected students.
  • Timeline for Processing Claims: For borrowers whose claims were still pending, the settlement established a strict timeline for the Department of Education to make decisions. Under the terms of the settlement, the Department was required to process claims within 18 to 24 months, depending on when the claim was submitted. If the Department failed to issue a decision within that timeframe, the borrower would automatically receive loan forgiveness.
  • Transparency and Accountability: Another critical component of the settlement was the requirement for the Department of Education to provide clearer explanations for denied claims. Borrowers whose claims were denied would receive a detailed explanation of the reasons for the denial, as well as information about how to appeal the decision.
  • No Recoupment from Borrowers: The settlement also included a provision that protected borrowers from future collections. Under this provision, the Department of Education was prohibited from attempting to recoup any loans that had been forgiven as part of the borrower defense process.

6. The Impact of Sweet v. Cardona on Borrower Defense

The Sweet v. Cardona settlement has had a profound impact on the Borrower Defense to Repayment program, restoring faith in a system that had been mired in delays and inefficiencies. For many borrowers, the settlement provided the long-awaited relief they had been seeking, allowing them to move forward without the crushing burden of student debt.

  • Restoring Trust in Borrower Defense: One of the most significant impacts of the settlement was the restoration of trust in the borrower defense process. Prior to the settlement, many borrowers felt abandoned by the Department of Education, as their claims languished without resolution. The settlement introduced accountability and deadlines, ensuring that borrowers would no longer be left in financial uncertainty.
  • Improved Efficiency: The timelines and transparency requirements established by the settlement have also improved the overall efficiency of the borrower defense program. By holding the Department to specific deadlines and requiring clear explanations for denials, the settlement has streamlined the process and reduced the backlog of pending claims.
  • Setting a Precedent for Future Claims: Sweet v. Cardona set an important legal precedent for future borrower defense claims. The case demonstrated that borrowers could hold the Department of Education accountable for failing to process claims in a timely and transparent manner. This precedent has empowered borrowers to advocate for their rights and seek legal recourse if their claims are wrongfully delayed or denied.

7. The Future of Borrower Defense

While the Sweet v. Cardona settlement represents a significant victory for student borrowers, the fight for borrower defense reform is far from over. The settlement addressed many of the immediate issues with the program, but it also highlighted the need for long-term reforms to prevent similar problems from arising in the future.

  • Ongoing Monitoring: As part of the settlement, the Department of Education is required to report on its progress in processing claims and to provide updates on the number of claims approved and denied. This ongoing monitoring is intended to ensure that the Department continues to meet its obligations under the settlement and that borrowers receive the relief they deserve.
  • Expanding Relief: In addition to processing pending claims, the Department of Education is also exploring ways to expand relief to other borrowers who may not have been included in the original settlement. This includes borrowers who attended schools that have since been found to have engaged in fraud or misconduct.
  • Advocacy for Broader Student Loan Reform: The Sweet v. Cardona case has also sparked renewed calls for broader student loan reform, particularly around the issue of student loan forgiveness for defrauded borrowers. Advocates are pushing for more comprehensive policies that would prevent predatory schools from taking advantage of students in the first place and would provide faster, more automatic relief to affected borrowers.

8. Conclusion

Sweet v. Cardona is a landmark case that has reshaped the Borrower Defense to Repayment program and provided critical relief for thousands of student borrowers. While the settlement represents a major step forward, it also underscores the need for continued reforms to ensure that borrowers are protected from fraudulent schools and can access the loan forgiveness they deserve.

For students who were defrauded by their schools, the Sweet v. Cardona case serves as a reminder that their voices matter and that legal recourse is available when federal agencies fail to fulfill their responsibilities. As the Department of Education continues to process claims and implement reforms, the legacy of Sweet v. Cardona will continue to shape the future of borrower defense and student loan policy in the United States.
